I clicked on a Kurzgesagt video the other night out of idle curiosity — “[The Rise of the Machines – Why Automation is Different this Time](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WSKi8HfcxEk).” I expected another familiar take on the AI wave crashing over 2026.

What I got was a cold splash of déjà vu.

The thesis was simple and brutal: past industrial revolutions eventually minted more jobs than they erased, but the Information Revolution plays by different rules. It prints wealth without printing work. Fewer hands build more value, and the humans left behind don’t get a second act. Mass displacement, the video warned, wouldn’t be a glitch in the transition — it *was* the transition.

Watching it, I felt the uncanny weight of recognition. I’m living through the full-scale rollout of Agentic AI across every industry I touch, with Physical AI — humanoid robots, embodied intelligence — already knocking on the door.

Then I glanced at the upload date.

**2017.**

Eight years ago, someone sketched the map of the world I’m standing in today, and most of us shrugged it off. That moment forced me to stop reacting to the present and start rethinking where, exactly, I want to stand in it.

## 1. Why the 2017 Thesis Aged Terrifyingly Well

Back then, the warning sounded alarmist. From where I sit in 2026, it reads like a forecast delivered too politely. Two reasons it holds up:

* **Productivity has decoupled from labor.** AI-native companies — Midjourney, Cursor, and a growing list behind them — are scaling into the hundreds of millions in revenue with headcounts you could fit in a single conference room. The “new jobs” exist, but not in the volumes needed to absorb the vanished ones. The math simply doesn’t balance anymore.
* **Adaptation cycles have collapsed.** The jump from “AI as a tool” to “AI as an agent” didn’t take a generation — it took a handful of quarters. Humans retrain on the scale of years. Models retrain on the scale of weekends. You can’t out-run a curve that steep; you have to ride it.

## 2. My Strategy: If You Can’t Beat Them, Engineer Their Arrival

My background sits squarely at the seam between machine learning and robotics, which means Kurzgesagt’s prediction doesn’t read to me as a threat. It reads as a blueprint for where my leverage lives.

**The daily practice: agent orchestration.** In 2026, prompting an AI is table stakes. The real work is directing autonomous agents through multi-step, high-stakes workflows — defining the problem, drawing the boundaries, auditing the output. I treat this as the floor, not the ceiling: it’s how I get leverage on every project I touch, not the thing I’m betting my career on.

**The bet: Physical AI.** The safer move would be to retreat into a traditional role and wait out the storm. I don’t believe that’s a real option. The storm *is* the weather now. So instead of hedging, I’m going all-in on Physical AI — and trying to be one of the people who guides its transition into the real world.

Here’s why this window matters. The new paradigm — embodied, learning-driven — is young enough that nobody has a decade of scar tissue in it yet. Veteran roboticists still have to re-learn how to think about data and policies. ML researchers still have to meet the brutal reality of actuators, latency, and friction. The gap between deep expertise and fast-moving newcomers is temporarily narrower than it will ever be again, and the people who can live at the seam between ML and robotics get to help define what “good” even looks like. Windows like that don’t stay open long. Once the playbook hardens, the usual hierarchies will snap back — and the people who got there first will be the ones writing them.

My plan is concrete. Track frontier research relentlessly. Learn the technical nuances of embodied AI not as an academic exercise but as an engineering one. When a breakthrough lands in a lab, be the one who drags it into production — who solves the unglamorous, brutal problems of scalability, reliability, and deployment.

If that sounds dramatic, it’s because it is. To borrow a figure from history: I want to be a modern-day Gutenberg for embodied intelligence. Gutenberg didn’t invent the ideas that reshaped Europe — the texts, the hunger for literacy, the slow intellectual thaw were already there. What he built was the machine that let those ideas *ship*, at scale, into the hands of people who would use them. That’s the role I want to play here. The breakthroughs will come out of the labs either way. The question is who engineers their transfer into the physical world — reliably, at scale, on terms that actually work. I’d like that to be me.

## Final Thoughts

Rewatching a “future” video from 2018 in 2026 is humbling in a very specific way. It reminds you that the pace of change is frightening, yes — but rarely as unpredictable as it feels in the moment. Someone usually saw it coming. The question is never really *whether* the machines rise; it’s what role you choose to play while they do.
